Engineering Portfolio

Praxis II Engineering Portfolio

My name is Nathan Xia, and I am an aspiring aerospace engineer, currently in my first year at the University of Toronto in the engineering science program. Within this design portfolio, I will provide an overview of my current approach to engineering design, as well as my utilization of various CTMFs (Concepts, Tools, Models, Frameworks) taught in Praxis and their connection to my updated engineering position statement. These tools have been embedded and integrated throughout my engineering design process and will be analyzed in three projects: the CIV102 Bridge Project, the Praxis I Design Report, and the Praxis II RFP. For each project, a "One-Pager" will be used to provide an overview, followed by an explanation of each CTMF, its impact on my current position, and a reflection on its usage.

Over the second semester of my first year, my approach to engineering design has become much more formalized. My stance at the start of the semester has developed from a general framework focused on research to a more formally defined process and philosophy. Specifically, while my approach remains rooted in a research context, I recognize that my value is more accurately defined as rigour, which is a refinement of my initial values of accuracy and resourcefulness.

To me, engineering design is an intelligent, iterative process used to develop solutions subject to constraints and expectations set by stakeholders. Within this definition, I want to emphasize the importance of intelligence and iteration, which I previously overlooked. To have an intelligent design process, one must formalize their design decisions and effectively justify their choices. To have an iterative process, one must go through multiple rounds of diverging, converging, and verification, acknowledging that the first option is very often the least impressive. All of these are representative of my value in engineering rigour and formality.

In my original position statement, I only highlighted the impact of past engineering projects, not real-world events. During the second semester, I realized many factors besides engineering experiences shaped my value of rigour. Even before engineering, I often chased perfection in school and extracurricular activities; my approach to engineering kept this pursuit, though it was reshaped. Praxis I Design Report

Praxis I Design Report

Subheading

One-Pager

Praxis I is an introductory course on the engineering process taken by engineering science students in the first semester of year one. It introduces students to a multitude of models and tools to aid in the engineering design process. It helps formalize the process through tools, concepts and definitions. To put the concepts taught in class to the test, students are tasked with coming up with a rigorous solution to an everyday inconvenience known as a "splartz" in teams of four. Each part must be formalized by defining its constraints, including its expectations, goals, and objectives. Furthermore, teams were required to come up with a multitude of prototypes and testing procedures to verify their concepts and converge onto the single most optimal design.

The chosen "splartz" aimed to solve whiteboard smudging for left-handed writers. We wanted a solution that had minimal impact to the natural writing style, whilst also preventing smudging entirely. Other key factors included not damaging the whiteboard, and ergonomics. These were all rigorously defined through our NGOs, which was used to formalize the engineering process.

Proxy tests were used to verify the validity of our prototypes. Many of our prototypes took great inspiration from reference designs. More information can be found linked in the original Design Brief. They are as follows:

  • "The Finger"
  • "The Fake-Out"
  • "The Eraser"
  • "The Platform"
  • "The Tape"

Specifically, "The Finger" took great inspiration from finger painting, and the designs surrounding that solution space. It works by having a small marker attached directly onto the finger tip, which allows the user to freely write on the whiteboard with the rest of their hand hovering above. Because this design comes from finger-painting, this style of writing is intuitive and does not impede upon writing speed or precision. The workings of this design can be clearly seen below.

Figure 2: Digital illustration of "The Finger" in use

The proxy tests used included:

  • Timed Writing Tests: Users were tasked with writing the alphabet and their speed was timed.
  • Smudging Tests: The percent amount of ink displaced after writing using the device was measured and compared.
  • Staining Tests: The Euclidean Difference in color between the whiteboard and the written area was measured and compared.

Figure 3: Picture of team member performing the Timed Writing Test

Ultimately, the Finger was chosen as the best design as it satisfied all our requirements and best met the outlined critical metrics. It did so because it left little to no visible stains on the test subject's hand or the whiteboard, and it did not negatively impact the readability of the writing produced by the design.

Because this was an earlier design project in my engineering journey, many of the aspects of this project helped to shape my current values and position, rather than following my current procedure. Despite this, many factors of this project were still intelligent and iterative, albeit less in comparison to the others. Aspects such as the use of NGOs as a framing tool, or taking inspiration from reference designs were intelligent and rigorous aspects of this engineering process.

However, the shortcomings of this project allowed me to realize the importance of rigour in engineering design. A major shortcoming within this process was the lack of iteration, specifically from the initial testing data to the final design. From this I began to follow a more formalized structure focusing on intelligent and iterative design rather than a generalized blanket procedure (as was my first position).

CTMF Analysis

Subheading

NGOs (Needs, Goals, Objectives) (Framing)

NGOs are the backbone of any formal engineering design. It formally outlines the goal of the design, and what a good solution should accomplish. It turns constraints and ideas into a rigid framework every design needs to follow. This formality ties directly into my updated engineering position. Using a tool such as this to define what needs to be accomplished, an intelligent and rigorous design process can begin.

The use of NGOs as a tool for framing proved to be extremely valuable during the design process in the context of Praxis I. Being able to turn something as inconsequential as a "splartz" into a real engineering problem with stakeholder, expectations, and constraints, aided the decision making and design process of the entire project. It helped formalize what a good design means, and what it should accomplish.

Figure 4: Snippet from design brief outlining the goals and objectives used to formalize the solution space

The pictured snippet outlines an example of an entry from the Praxis I design brief. It is split into a clear goal, as well as measurable objectives justified through secondary sources from standards and codes. Every aspect of this is an intelligent step in engineering design. The NGOs are the cornerstone of any framing stage in engineering. It is extremely effective at formalizing a problem, and it helps guide the engineer into the right direction of a final design. It is useful in all contexts, but is especially crucial in turning an abstract problem or idea into a rigid framework that needs to be solved.

Subheading

Cognitive Bias (Converging)

Cognitive bias is a psychological phenomenon that impacts an individual's decisions unbeknownst to the individual. It is a blanket term that describes many flaws in individual reasoning, many of which cloud judgement. Understanding how this concept affects team design decisions significantly impacted my current position in engineering. Overlooking this factor greatly delayed our team's ability to converge and a singular design. From this, I began to see the value in formalizing every decision, and to eliminate these biases from impacting design features.

Being able to recognize cognitive bias as perception error before convergence is a crucial step in ensuring fair and effective dialogue between team members in the converging process. In Praxis I, each team member had their own initial preferential prototype. Recognizing this initial preconceived bias early would have been a crucial step in moving forward and eliminating prototypes and designs. This bias can be seen in the free writing excerpts written by two different team members, both of which decided that their own design was superior to the others.

Figure 5: Snippet from free writing activity outlining the difference in opinion from two team members

Being able to use this tool and concept before converging is extremely effective and eliminating designs that are objectively inferior, rather than simply deciding based on bias. Although this tool should be used in design contexts prior to convergence, it is especially useful in team situations where each individual may hold cognitive bias, influencing their argumentation and decision making regarding the project.

Subheading

Proxy Tests (Converging)

Proxy tests are a type of verification procedure that copies official tests, oftentimes with less resources. However, the core testing procedure and practice is copied thus providing results that are up to par with industry standards. Using industry standard testing procedures ties directly into my current position as an engineer in a similar manner to how reference designs do. Because it borrows from existing terminology, the justification for using this test is largely covered already. This once again ties into the intelligence aspect of engineering design, which is a key concept in my position as an engineer.

In Praxis I, proxy tests were used to converge. The use of proxy tests, to mimic official tests, proved to be a valuable tool to further converge down from the initial 5 prototypes. Specifically, a relative assessment was used between prototypes to judge them, rather than an absolute assessment which could not be done without more data. Copying official testing proved to give data that is significantly more telling than any testing procedure us as a team could have come up with, which is one of the many strong suits of copying a test to create a proxy test.

During the verification process and further converging process, the use of proxy tests to gather data is extremely effective in eliminating designs to a single one. A relative assessment is also just as effective, as you are comparing these designs to each other, which is relative. The use of these tools and concepts are especially useful during initial testing phases where the lack of resources and time prevents official testing with absolute assessments. Official testing may be more useful on a single design, after initial proxy testing and relative assessments converged down.

Subheading

Reference Designs (Diverging)

A reference design is defined as an already established design used to solve a similar or related problem. It is often used for inspiration and as a starting point for many design challenges. The usage of reference designs ties into my position as an engineer significantly. Iterating and modifying an existing design is much easier than creating and validating a novel solution. Furthermore, justification for a design based on a reference is much easier to formulate as it borrows from a proven design. Both of these factors tie into my value of rigour in engineering design, as well as my previously defined definition.

In the context of Praxis II, this tool was used to converge down to the final design. Many reference designs were consulted such as the GMX 7 and waterwheel/rowing machine. These each independently branched off into their own prototypes that were showcased at various levels of fidelity.

The significance of reference designs in this engineering design process was quite significant as many of our prototypes borrowed these reference designs. For example, a rowing machine that uses air pressure was directly modified to use water, which was made when considering our stakeholders, and values as a team in safety/simplicity.

Figure 7: Reference design that inspired the waterwheel prototype to create variable resistance. More information can be found here.

Figure 8: Reference design (GMX7) that inspired the final design (Zipline)

This same mechanism pictured is used in one of the final four prototypes to generate resistance. As for the GMX7, although the internal mechanism is novel, the premise of having a two roped system and a "guiding rope" to create resistance greatly inspired the design of the zipline. The use of reference designs proved to be very valuable in this design process, as it provided a launchpad for ideas to be generated from. This tool is best utilized in the diverging phase, when tasked with creating a better solution to an engineer problem, rather than a completely novel design.

Subheading

Primary Research (Framing)

Primary research is the act of gathering information directly from the source itself. This could be from interviews, surveys or personal statements. In praxis, primary research usually comes from surveys or interviews from members of the community directly. This differs from secondary sources which are already processed to some extent. This connects to my position as primary research often leaves less room for interpretation over the demands of the stakeholders, which thus provides a more rigorous and airtight constraint, leading to a better design.

To ensure that our design met the constraints of the engineering problem, Praxis II differed from the rest of the engineering projects in that direct primary research was conducted with the community itself. A series of questions to the community were sent out in the late stages of the process to validate our converged engineering design. These Q and A responses helped us build confidence in the effectiveness of our final design, as it prevented speculation and generalities that may have been incorrect.

Figure 9: Snippet from the Q and A sent to the community

As a whole, however, this tool has some major drawbacks in comparison to other methods of research, as it relies on the willingness of the other party to respond. In this case, the response time of the original community was delayed, and thus was insignificant in guiding the actual design process instead of just validation. These late responses can significantly hinder progress, especially on the development of a solution that has time constraints as many do.

Subheading

Pairwise Comparison (Converging)

The pairwise is a converging tool used to directly compare a variety of designs against each other. It involves creating a table of n x n entries, for n number of designs. For each entry, either a 1 or a 0 is added depending on the winning design. In the end, the rows are tallied and the highest number is the best solution. Using a formalized and structured tool like this helps one organize their thoughts into a cohesive output rather than a baseless opinion. This ties into the intelligence aspect of engineering design.

The pairwise comparison tool proved to be especially useful in this engineering process as well. This tool was used many times throughout the process at varying converging stages. For example, it was used to converge down to the final 4 prototypes, as well as to converge down to the optimal design direction for the chosen prototype.

Figure 10: Snippet from showcase showing an example usage of pairwise comparison to converge down variable resistance designs

This tool proved to be very effective at organizing the team's thoughts and finalizing a decision. It helps to formalize the converging process, marking direct winners and losers for each comparison. It's best used to determine the preliminary best solution when considering multiple options, prior to considering testing data, and validation in later stages. Personally, I find this convergence technique much more effective in comparison to pugh charts and other converging tools, as it allows for direct comparison using one table, whereas pugh charts require a chart for every comparison. This conciseness and formal structure ties back into my position as an engineer, who values simplicity and formalities in engineering design.

Structural Design

CIV102 Bridge Project

Subheading

One-Pager

CIV102 is an introductory class to engineering design and civil engineering taken by Engineering Science students in the first semester of year one. Its purpose is to teach students good engineering practice and techniques in the context of civil engineering. Throughout the course, students are taught various concepts such as: factors of safety (FOS), modes of failure, bending moment diagrams and shear force diagrams. At the end of the semester, students are tasked with putting their knowledge on display by designing their own bridge cross section, made entirely out of contact cement and matboard. Each team of three is constrained to one 813mm x 1016mm x 1.27mm sheet of Matboard, and two 30mL tubes of contact cement to work with. The purchasing of more materials is prohibited. These bridges are put to the test with increasing amounts of weight in the form of "trains" to determine their strength. These trains provide force at various contact points, increasing the complexity of the engineering problem.

Figure 11: Diagram outlining the train and mass used to test the bridges

Figure 12: Diagram outlining the loading procedure during testing

Exact testing details, however, were hidden from the class until the day of testing, to prevent any trivial solutions that play on any flaws in the testing procedure itself. Scoring was done based on the strength to weight ratio. It is important to note that for this project, the problem and expectations were already formally defined, and so no additional framing was required during the engineering design process. Due to the staggering amount of calculations beginning the design process and iterating, students were encouraged to simulate designs through scripts. These scripts were used to make graphs, and predict failure modes, ultimately determining the direction for iteration. An emphasis on intelligent iterative design practices.

Figure 13: Shear force diagram produced from code as train moves along the bridge.

After iterating through numerous reference designs, a PI beam design was chosen as the optimal cross sectional diagram as it plays into the two weakest points in the matboard. It also allows for more diaphragms to be placed into the design, which help account for gaps in the equations. Furthermore, the simplistic design requires the least folds and cuts, which gives more tolerance for errors in construction that could impact the performance of the build in real world testing. However, the construction proved to be more daunting of a task than expected, and long hours clouded judgement during construction. All of these factors impacted the final results of the testing, which were suboptimal. Mentions differ in code prediction output.

Figure 14: Final bridge cross section using a PI beam design

Figure 15: Completed bridge construction

This entire procedure, with the exception of the framing aspect, follows my defined engineering process very closely. Every design was backed by reference designs, and every decision was one made using intelligence in that it was properly justified. Iteration was also a key aspect of this project, as we went through ~10-20 iterations for each design before determining the optimal choice. Rigour was a key motivator in every aspect of this engineering process.

Functional Prototyping (Represent)

Functional prototyping is a subtype of prototypes that is specifically made for verification. In comparison to other forms of prototypes like those to convey ideas, functional prototypes must be catered to produce data to be tested. In this case, the functional prototype is represented as code in a program, which can easily be tested in the simulation. Once again, creating a functional prototype specifically helps significantly with testing and iteration. Each iteration slowly improves the final design, tying into my value of rigour as an engineer.

The most significant difference between this engineering design project and the others is that the problem and expectations (framing) had already been completed, and so more time and effort could be spent on representing and converging on a final design. For example, the use of code as a form of prototyping and representation was very apparent throughout the engineering process. Code was written out to simulate how each iteration of the prototype would fare against the various testing conditions. From there, we were able to see exactly the point of failure and how to alter the design to strengthen that area.

Figure 16: Excerpt from the code used to calculate and represent prototype cross sectional properties

The code snippet pictured shows the way the design is represented. It is encoded in a series of variables which correspond to dimensions, as well as properties of the shape such as the moment of inertia and centroid.

This tool of prototyping through simulations instead of through physical models proved to be very effective in the engineering process. This is because these prototypes were built directly for verification and testing, which allowed for very quick iterations between designs and stages. In an engineering problem where many rounds of testing are required in quick succession, creating a prototype that is tailored to verification is crucial in meeting the constraints of the engineering problem.

Subheading

Simulation Verification (Converging)

Verification is the procedure of testing prototypes, and to produce data for comparison. It is used to both ensure the design meets the requirements set, but is also used to definitively determine the optimal design when using an evaluating criteria. Verification can be done using a variety of methods, such as proxy tests, or through simulation analysis. In this specific example, verification through simulations/equations were chosen to be the most effective. Similar to the previous CTMF, this was chosen to allow time for more iterations, once again improving engineering rigour.

Specifically, verification was done through code simulations, which work hand in hand with the prototypes used. The same verification methods were used on each prototype, to quickly determine the performance of each design. For a project that requires a final design to be built and implemented fully, such as a bridge, numerous stages of verification are required, and so verification through code simulations is the best choice to automate the process.

Figure 17: Excerpt from the code used to calculate modes of failure and factors of safety for each design

Evidently, the code snippet pictures displays a function that calculates all the modes of failure for each bridge design and given dimensions. This was how designs were verified and compared to each other. Despite this, this verification method fell short in many areas, as it does not consider the human factors of implementation and construction of the bridge. Furthermore, there were some modes of failure that the code could not accurately predict, leaving the decision solely in the judgement of the designer. While this tool is extremely valuable for rapid prototyping and iterations, it's best to use other forms of verification to prepare for human error.

Subheading

Build/Implementation (Represent)

Building and implementation is a key part of the represent strand, and it is the act of actually bringing all of the previous iterations into reality. For example, it may be integrating a design into a community, or sharing a design with the world through publication. A significant part of this project was implementing and building this process based on all of the simulation data and prototyping. By representing our final design as a constructed piece, we were able to see our design serve the purpose that we were designing for. In this case, this purpose was to support the most weight with the lowest mass under the specific loading conditions.

Building and implementing a blueprint of the design into real life proved to be much more difficult than initially expected, and the time cost of this process impacted the outcome significantly. Uneven cuts, faulty glueing, and awkward folds all played a role in reducing the strength of the completed bridge. This concept of building and implementation is a necessity, however, it may prove to be more useful in other design contexts with more formal machinery and construction equipment.
