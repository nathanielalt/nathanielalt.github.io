Engineering Portfolio

Praxis II Engineering Portfolio

My name is Nathan Xia, and I am an aspiring aerospace engineer, currently in my first year at the University of Toronto in the engineering science program. Within this design portfolio, I will provide an overview of my current approach to engineering design, as well as my utilization of various CTMFs (Concepts, Tools, Models, Frameworks) taught in Praxis and their connection to my updated engineering position statement. These tools have been embedded and integrated throughout my engineering design process and will be analyzed in three projects: the CIV102 Bridge Project, the Praxis I Design Report, and the Praxis II RFP. For each project, a “One-Pager” will be used to provide an overview, followed by an explanation of each CTMF, its impact on my current position, and a reflection on its usage.

Over the second semester of my first year, my approach to engineering design has become much more formalized. My stance at the start of the semester has developed from a general framework focused on research to a more formally defined process and philosophy. Specifically, while my approach remains rooted in a research context, I recognize that my value is more accurately defined as rigour, which is a refinement of my initial values of accuracy and resourcefulness.

To me, engineering design is an intelligent, iterative process used to develop solutions subject to constraints and expectations set by stakeholders. Within this definition, I want to emphasize the importance of intelligence and iteration, which I previously overlooked. To have an intelligent design process, one must formalize their design decisions and effectively justify their choices. To have an iterative process, one must go through multiple rounds of diverging, converging, and verification, acknowledging that the first option is very often the least impressive. All of these are representative of my value in engineering rigour and formality.

In my original position statement, I only highlighted the impact of past engineering projects, not real-world events. During the second semester, I realized many factors besides engineering experiences shaped my value of rigour. Even before engineering, I often chased perfection in school and extracurricular activities; my approach to engineering kept this pursuit, though it was reshaped. Praxis I Design Report

Praxis I Design Report

Subheading

One-Pager

Praxis I is an introductory course on the engineering process taken by engineering science students in the first semester of year one. It introduces students to a multitude of models and tools to aid in the engineering design process. It helps formalize the process through tools, concepts and definitions. To put the concepts taught in class to the test, students are tasked with coming up with a rigorous solution to an everyday inconvenience known as a “splartz” in teams of four. Each part must be formalized by defining its constraints, including its expectations, goals, and objectives. Furthermore, teams were required to come up with a multitude of prototypes and testing procedures to verify their concepts and converge onto the single most optimal design.

The chosen “splartz” aimed to solve whiteboard smudging for left-handed writers. We wanted a solution that had minimal impact to the natural writing style, whilst also preventing smudging entirely. Other key factors included not damaging the whiteboard, and ergonomics. These were all rigorously defined through our NGOs, which was used to formalize the engineering process.

Proxy tests were used to verify the validity of our prototypes. Many of our prototypes took great inspiration from reference designs. More information can be found linked in the original Design Brief. They are as follows:

  • "The Finger"
  • "The Fake-Out"
  • "The Eraser"
  • "The Platform"
  • "The Tape"

Specifically, "The Finger" took great inspiration from finger painting, and the designs surrounding that solution space. It works by having a small marker attached directly onto the finger tip, which allows the user to freely write on the whiteboard with the rest of their hand hovering above. Because this design comes from finger-painting, this style of writing is intuitive and does not impede upon writing speed or precision. The workings of this design can be clearly seen below.

Figure 2: Digital illustration of "The Finger" in use

The proxy tests used included:

  • Timed Writing Tests: Users were tasked with writing the alphabet and their speed was timed.
  • Smudging Tests: The percent amount of ink displaced after writing using the device was measured and compared.
  • Staining Tests: The Euclidean Difference in color between the whiteboard and the written area was measured and compared.

Figure 3: Picture of team member performing the Timed Writing Test

Ultimately, the Finger was chosen as the best design as it satisfied all our requirements and best met the outlined critical metrics. It did so because it left little to no visible stains on the test subject’s hand or the whiteboard, and it did not negatively impact the readability of the writing produced by the design.

Because this was an earlier design project in my engineering journey, many of the aspects of this project helped to shape my current values and position, rather than following my current procedure. Despite this, many factors of this project were still intelligent and iterative, albeit less in comparison to the others. Aspects such as the use of NGOs as a framing tool, or taking inspiration from reference designs were intelligent and rigorous aspects of this engineering process.

However, the shortcomings of this project allowed me to realize the importance of rigour in engineering design. A major shortcoming within this process was the lack of iteration, specifically from the initial testing data to the final design. From this I began to follow a more formalized structure focusing on intelligent and iterative design rather than a generalized blanket procedure (as was my first position).

Subheading

One-Pager

Praxis II builds on the concepts taught in Praxis I and allows the students to put their knowledge to practice solving real issues facing communities around the GTA (Greater Toronto Area). Students are able to choose a specific opportunity, and create solutions using the engineering tools taught in class. These designs are then presented to the community and have the potential to be fully implemented.

The design process within this project followed my updated position statement much closer, focusing on rigour and iterative design in every step.
